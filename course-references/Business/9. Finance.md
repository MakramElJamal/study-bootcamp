Break -even analysis is a financial calculation used to determine the point at which a business's revenue exactly covers its total costs, meaning the business neither makes a profit nor incurs a loss.

This analysis is crucial for any business because it helps determine the minimum performance necessary to ensure the business is financially viable.

KEY COMPONENTS OF BREAKEVEN ANALYSIS

Fixed Costs: These are costs that do not change with the level of production or sales volume. Examples include rent, salaries, and insurance.

2.Variable Costs: These costs vary directly with the level of production or sales. Examples include materials and direct labor.

Sales Price per Unit: The amount charged to customers for each unit sold.

Contribution Margin per Unit: This is calculated by subtracting the variable cost per unit from the sales price per unit. It represents the portion of each sale that contributes to covering fixed costs.

CALCULATION

The break -even point (BEP) can be calculated in units (how many units need to be sold) and in sales dollars (how much revenue needs to be generated):

Break -even Point in Units: BEP (units)=Fixed Costs/ Contribution per unit

Where the contribution margin per unit is (Sales Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit).

Break -even Point in Sales Dollars: BEP (sales dollars)=BEP (units)×Sales Price per

Alternatively, it can be calculated directly using the ratio of fixed costs to the contribution margin ratio

BEP (sales dollars)=Fixed Costs/Contribution Margin Ratio

Where the contribution margin ratio is (Contribution Margin per Unit / Sales Price per Unit).

EXAMPLE

Suppose a company sells a product for $100 each, with variable costs of $60 per unit and total fixed costs of $20,000.

The contribution margin per unit would be $40 ($100 -$60).

The break -even point in units would be: BEP (units)=$20,000/ $40=500 units

This means the company needs to sell 500 units to cover all its costs. Any sales beyond 500 units will contribute to profit.

PROFIT

Profit, or net income, is the amount of money a company earns after subtracting all its expenses from its total revenue.

It is a key indicator of the company's profitability and is reported on the income statement. Profit can be broken down into several categories:

Gross Profit: Revenue minus the cost of goods sold.

Operating Profit: Gross profit minus all operating expenses.

Net Profit: Operating profit minus all other non-operating expenses, interest, and taxes.

1. Gross Profit Margin

Formula: Gross Profit / Revenue × 100

Purpose: Measures the percentage of revenue that exceeds the cost of goods sold (COGS).

2. Net Profit Margin

Formula: Net Income / Revenue × 100

Purpose: Indicates how much of each dollar of revenues is translated into profits.

3. Return on Investment (ROI)

Formula: Net Profit / Investment × 100

Purpose: Used to evaluate the efficiency of an investment or to compare the efficiencies of several different investments.

FINANCIAL RATIOS -LIQUIDITY

1. Current Ratio

The current ratio measures a company's ability to cover its short-term liabilities with its short-term assets. It's calculated by dividing the company's current assets by its current liabilities.

Formula: Current Ratio=Current Assets / Current Liabilities

A current ratio greater than 1 indicates that the company has more assets than liabilities due within a year, suggesting good short-term financial health. A ratio under 1 suggests that the company may struggle to meet its short-term obligations.

FINANCIAL RATIOS -LIQUIDITY

2. Cash Ratio

The cash ratio is the most conservative liquidity ratio. It measures a company's ability to settle short -term liabilities with cash and cash equivalents alone, excluding receivables, inventory, and other current assets.

Formula: Cash Ratio=Cash and Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities

This ratio indicates how well a company can pay off its current liabilities without having to sell or liquidate other assets.
