COST PLUS PRICING

Also known as markup pricing, involves calculating the total cost of producing a product and then adding a fixed percentage markup to determine the selling price.

Cost of Production: Calculate the direct and indirect costs involved in producing the product (e.g., materials, labor, overhead).

Markup: Add a percentage markup to the cost to ensure profit.

Selling Price: Cost + Markup = Selling Price.

VALUE -BASED PRICING

Value -based pricing sets prices primarily based on the perceived value of the product to the customer rather than on the cost of production.

Focuses on what customers are willing to pay based on the benefits and utility they perceive.

Perceived Value: Understand the value the product provides to customers, such as time savings, improved performance, or prestige.

Customer Willingness to Pay: Determine how much customers are willing to pay for the perceived value.

Set Price: Price is set based on customer perception, which may be higher or lower than the cost-plus pricing approach.

PENETRATION PRICING

Penetration pricing involves setting a low price initially to attract customers and gain market share quickly. Once the product has gained traction, the price is gradually increased.

· Low Initial Price: Set the price low to attract a large number of customers. Market Share Growth: As the product gains market share, customer loyalty builds. Gradual Price Increase: Raise prices once a

significant customer base is established.

SKIMMING PRICE

The opposite of penetration pricing, where a high price is set initially to maximize profits from customers willing to pay a premium. The price is then gradually lowered to attract more price-sensitive customers.

Ex: a new release of IPhone: High Initial Price: Launch the product at a high price, targeting early adopters who are less pricesensitive.

Price Reductions: Gradually lower the price to reach a broader market as competition increases or as the product becomes more widely known.

PHYCOLOGICAL PRICING

Psychological pricing involves setting prices that have a psychological impact on consumers, such as pricing something at $9.99 instead of $10 to make it seem less expensive.

Perceived Value: Prices are set in a way that makes them more attractive or affordable in the eyes of the customer.

Pricing Tactics: Common tactics include charm pricing (e.g., $9.99), prestige pricing (higher prices to suggest quality), and bundling (combining products at a reduced price).
